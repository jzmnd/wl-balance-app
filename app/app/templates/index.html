Work-Life Balance Prediction Project

Jeremy Smith

Work-life balance is inherently linked to stress and stress related illness, as well as a lack of human productivity. Therefore the ability to predict a person's propensity to overwork or have a lack of leisure or self-care time etc. is very important. The healthcare industry already uses patient categorization to offer better tailored health services and more personalized care. The addition of work-life balance metrics to this would be extremely useful for improvng this categorization.

How do we define work life balance? I have chosen 5 metrics that can be easily calculated from activity times that give an indication of a person's work life balance. The first 3 are scores - the higher the better. The last two classify groups by a daily cut-off time, first for paid work and second for unpaid work (e.g. housework, childcare).

Metric 1

Weighted difference between "life actvities" and "paid work activities".
This metric is based on several key activity times related to work-life balance and scaled to be a score between 0 and 1.

Metric 2

Weighted difference between "life actvities" and "unpaid work activities".
As for Metric 1 but with unpaid rather than paid work activities.

Metric 3

Percentage of day spent on Personal Care.
Here we simply calculate the time spent on personal care activities (including sleep) as a percentage of the day.

Metric 4

Percentage of day spent on Leisure/Socializing.
Here we simply calculate time spent on leisure, social or relaxation activities as a percentage of the day.

Metric 5

More than 9 hours of work in the day?
This categorizes a respondent by whether they work a "long" day or not.

Metric 6

More than 4 hours of unpaid work in the day?
This categorizes a respondent by whether they spend a significant amount of time on childcare, housework etc.

All data is from the American Time Use Survey (ATUS) that is published by the US Bureau for Labor Statistics. Daily activity times for respondents between 2003 and 2015.
